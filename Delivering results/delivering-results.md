## Introduction
### Dave Ulrich

This is a great time for those interested in human resources. Human resource issues have become central to business deliberations, surfacing in boardrooms where executives plan and in conference rooms where managers act. Discussions often seek answers to such questions as:

- How do we fight and win the war for talent, both at the leadership level and throughout the firm? How do we invest in and receive a return on intellectual capital equal to or greater than economic capital?

- How do we create an organization with speed that adapts, changes, transforms, and reduces cycle time?

- How do we facilitate learning by both the individual and the organization?

- How do we build a culture of commitment and contribution to create an organization with an unrelenting focus on customers?

- How do we build leaders in depth within an organization to sustain one generation of leadership to the next?

- How do we build connections to ensure that we can work with global customers, distribution channels, and suppliers?

- How do we create policies and practices to ensure that people are treated with dignity and that diversity in race, gender, and thought is respected?

These are people questions. They are human resource questions. They are questions in which both academicians and practitioners are interested. And they are questions central to business competitiveness.

This book offers a unique perspective on human resource management. It proposes that human resource practices and practitioners can and should focus on deliverables or outcomes of their work rather than on doing their work better. Rather than focusing on what HR does, this book frames human resource work by what it delivers.

This distinction represents an important change in HR thinking, moving the focus from activity to outcome, from process to deliverable. To emphasize a metaphor I have used elsewhere, rather than seeing an architect as merely ordering materials, we see the architect as someone who creates a building that will serve an organization's needs.

Using a results focus as the lens through which to examine HR work changes how we think about and practice the profession. It challenges each HR professional to define the deliverables of his or her work. The HR professional is like the architect who does not merely have a blueprint, but has a building.

A results focus on human resources means that results rather than activities become the criteria by which human resources work is judged. There are at least four deliverables for HR that become the standard by which the profession is assessed: strategic clarity, making change happen, creating intellectual capital, and building organizational capability.

### Organizational Capability: Be an Organization Champion

Organizational capability represents what the organization is known for, what it is good at, and how it patterns activities to deliver value. Thinking of organization capability can be broken into a useful metaphor: The organization is like a building, and the leaders of that organization are its architects. Organizational audit helps leaders identify the capabilities currently residing within the firm and defines what new capabilities will be required to respond to future business requirements.

Organizations have many capabilities, some of which have been identified through research. For example, these include speed, meaning that an organization can make change happen rapidly; accountability, meaning that individuals within an organization know and perform their roles with discipline; learning, meaning that an organization innovates and generates new ideas with impact; and collaboration across boundaries, meaning that an organization efficiently manages information and ideas across time, geographic, and structural boundaries.

### Strategic Clarity: Be a Strategic Partner

Strategic clarity, the second HR deliverable, ensures that the firm makes strategic choices and turns those choices into organizational action. Strategic clarity exists when managers throughout a firm understand and share the strategy and when customers and investors can express what makes the firm unique or different. HR professionals deliver strategy by making the strategic processes happen, by turning strategic plans into organizational practices, and by becoming full business partners in building an organization that accomplishes its goals.
