## Introduction (HBR Excerpt)

Leaders often behave as if they agree on why, what, and how to change—when they actually don’t. That’s why most transformation efforts fail.

Decades of experience and research have consistently shown that most organizational change efforts fail. In 1993 Michael Hammer, who launched the business-process-reengineering movement, somberly concluded in his book *Reengineering the Corporation: A Manifesto for Business Revolution* that “as many as 50% to 70% of the organizations that undertake a reengineering effort do not achieve the dramatic results they intended.”

## Key Concepts (Based on BCG Research)

*Note: This content is based on the authors' BCG research and book "How Change Really Works", which forms the basis for the HBR article.*

### Principle: Get True Agreement, Not False Alignment

Too often, executive teams embark on a transformation before they truly agree on why change is needed, what those changes are, and how they will occur. When people behave as if they are more agreed than they really are, we say that they are in a state of **false alignment**. What they should seek instead is **true agreement**.

#### Three Common Consequences of False Alignment:
1. **Paralysis:** The organization is unable to move forward because of unacknowledged friction at the top.
2. **Hyperactivity:** Teams start multiple, often conflicting initiatives to try and satisfy different unaligned leader visions.
3. **Tunnel Vision:** Leaders focus only on their own siloed objectives, missing the broader transformation goals.

#### Three Common Causes of False Alignment:
*   **Lack of awareness:** Executives don’t realize that they don’t agree.
*   **Conflict avoidance:** Leaders pretend to agree to avoid difficult conversations or interpersonal tension.
*   **Procrastination:** Executives believe they can resolve their differences later in the process.

#### How to Reach True Agreement:
1. **Set clear parameters:** Define exactly what needs to be decided.
2. **Provoke an early exchange:** Surface differences early rather than letting them fester.
3. **Have a quality debate:** Ensure all viewpoints are heard and challenged.
4. **Come to a formal verdict:** Make a definitive decision.
5. **Send a unified message:** Once a decision is made, all leaders must support it publicly.

#### Options for Handling Strongly Held Disagreements:
*   **Try disagreeing again:** Re-examine the data and logic.
*   **Subtract or defer:** Remove or delay the contentious parts of the change program.
*   **Offer an exit:** Provide an attractive way for those who fundamentally disagree to leave the transformation or organization.
*   **Proceed despite disagreement:** As a last resort, move forward with a clear mandate, though this is the riskiest path.

## Summary of "How Change Really Works" Principles
Successful business transformation is knowable, repeatable, and rooted in the science of behavior change. Beyond alignment, the authors identify other critical principles:
*   **Increase agency, not just involvement:** Give employees the capacity to make choices.
*   **Expect take-up to be earned:** Adoption isn't automatic; it must be motivated.
*   **Understand emotions through feedback:** Use data, not just instinct, to gauge how people feel.
*   **Use rituals:** Establish processes that build habits.
*   **Share stories and symbols:** Use non-financial motivators.
*   **Create momentum throughout:** Don't just focus on the start.
